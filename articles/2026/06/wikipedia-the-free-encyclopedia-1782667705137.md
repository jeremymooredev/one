## From today's featured article

[![Glider flight by Laurence J. Lesh at Morris Park](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Lawrence_J._Lesh_in_Glider_Flight_at_Morris_Park.png/250px-Lawrence_J._Lesh_in_Glider_Flight_at_Morris_Park.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lawrence_J._Lesh_in_Glider_Flight_at_Morris_Park.png)

Glider flight by Laurence J. Lesh at Morris Park

The **[Morris Park Aerodrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morris_Park_Aerodrome "Morris Park Aerodrome")** was a short-lived [airfield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerodrome "Aerodrome") in what is now the [Morris Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morris_Park,_Bronx "Morris Park, Bronx") section of [the Bronx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Bronx "The Bronx"), New York City. In operation from 1908 to 1909, it was the first flying field in the nation, occupying the grounds of the defunct [Morris Park Racecourse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morris_Park_Racecourse "Morris Park Racecourse"). The [Aeronautical Society of New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aeronautical_Society_of_New_York "Aeronautical Society of New York") leased the land in 1908, using it for building and testing aircraft, and for putting on public exhibitions including major events in November 1908 and June 1909. The first event, captured in an oil painting by [Rudolph Dirks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolph_Dirks "Rudolph Dirks") titled *The Fledglings*, included several glider flights by sixteen-year-old [Laurence Lesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laurence_J._Lesh "Laurence J. Lesh") *(pictured)*, culminating in a crash in which he was severely injured. The second event had flights by [Glenn Curtiss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glenn_Curtiss "Glenn Curtiss") in *[Golden Flyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curtiss_No._1 "Curtiss No. 1")*, his motorized [biplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biplane "Biplane"), including the first demonstration of a stable flight around a closed course using [ailerons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aileron "Aileron") for lateral control. When the aerodrome closed for residential development, the Society moved its operations to [Roosevelt Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roosevelt_Field_\(airport\) "Roosevelt Field (airport)") on [Long Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_Island "Long Island"). *(**[Full article...](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morris_Park_Aerodrome "Morris Park Aerodrome")**)*

## Today's featured picture

[![Genevieve Clark Thomson](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Genevieve_Clark_%28Thomson%29_-_Bain_News_Service_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Genevieve_Clark_%28Thomson%29_-_Bain_News_Service_%28cropped%29.jpg)](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Genevieve_Clark_(Thomson)_-_Bain_News_Service_(cropped).jpg>)

**[Genevieve Clark Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genevieve_Clark_Thomson "Genevieve Clark Thomson")** (1894–1981) was an American [suffragist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suffrage "Suffrage"), journalist and political candidate. The daughter of [Champ Clark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Champ_Clark "Champ Clark"), the [speaker of the US House of Representatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives "Speaker of the United States House of Representatives"), she was educated in Washington, DC, and worked as a reporter from 1913. In 1915 she married publisher [James M. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_M._Thomson_\(newspaper_publisher\) "James M. Thomson (newspaper publisher)"), whom she had met while campaigning for her father's presidential nomination. A supporter of [temperance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperance_movement "Temperance movement") and the [Woman's Christian Temperance Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woman%27s_Christian_Temperance_Union "Woman's Christian Temperance Union"), Thomson was active in the women's suffrage movement. In 1924 she ran unsuccessfully for [Louisiana's 2nd congressional district](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana%27s_2nd_congressional_district "Louisiana's 2nd congressional district") seat in Congress, losing to J. Zach Spearing. This photograph shows Thomson using a [candlestick telephone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candlestick_telephone "Candlestick telephone") in around 1910–1915.
